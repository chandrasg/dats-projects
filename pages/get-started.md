# Get Started

Follow these steps to enroll in DATS 5990 (Practicum) or DATS 9990 (Thesis).

## Before You Begin

<div class="info-box">
Students typically pursue a thesis or practicum in their 2nd year (2nd or 3rd semester). You should complete at least one elective course in your project's application domain before starting. Before writing your proposal, read 2–3 past reports from the <a href="{{ '/resources/' | relative_url }}">Resources page</a> — they're the best way to calibrate what a realistic scope looks like.
</div>

## Enrollment Steps

<ol class="steps">
<li>
<strong>Identify a faculty advisor</strong><br>
Find a Penn faculty member willing to supervise your project. Two approaches that work well: (1) approach a faculty member whose course you took and whose expertise aligns with your interests, or (2) search "upenn research &lt;your domain&gt;" and email faculty in your area. See the <a href="https://docs.google.com/document/d/1iIi1KfWK8GDqGP3Bpz3bU5oN1zaEhs4IBiDr_JG84G4/edit" target="_blank">DATS Focus Areas Handbook</a> for campus research groups. When reaching out cold, briefly describe your background, what you've read of their work, and what you'd like to explore — one short paragraph is enough.
</li>
<li>
<strong>Define your project scope</strong><br>
Discuss the topic and scope with your advisor. Identify the research questions you'll explore and your specific role. For a thesis, you should lead the project end-to-end. For a practicum, your contribution can be a distinct piece of a larger effort. See <strong>What Makes a Good Project</strong> below for guidance on scope.
</li>
<li>
<strong>Prepare your proposal</strong><br>
Write a 2-page project proposal. A strong proposal covers five things:
<ul>
<li><strong>Problem statement:</strong> What question are you answering, and why does it matter?</li>
<li><strong>Background:</strong> What already exists, and where is the gap?</li>
<li><strong>Your approach:</strong> What will you do, and what is your specific role?</li>
<li><strong>Success criteria:</strong> How will you know the project succeeded?</li>
<li><strong>Timeline:</strong> Key milestones mapped to the semester calendar.</li>
</ul>
The proposal doesn't need to be final — it's a planning tool and a scope agreement between you and your advisor.
</li>
<li>
<strong>Get the advisor agreement signed</strong><br>
Download the <a href="https://drive.google.com/file/d/19d3zkQQ5TE014Onx_T0yOSQOgVGeGfKO/view" target="_blank">Advisor Agreement Form</a>, have your faculty advisor sign it, and keep the signed copy ready for upload.
</li>
<li>
<strong>Submit the project form</strong><br>
Fill out the <a href="https://forms.gle/fYeA2nXtkgzqLpio6" target="_blank">Project/Thesis Submission Form</a>. You'll upload your signed advisor agreement and project proposal as part of this form.
</li>
<li>
<strong>Request the course in Path@Penn</strong><br>
Request either <strong>DATS 5990</strong> (Practicum) or <strong>DATS 9990</strong> (Thesis) in Path@Penn. Once approved, Staci Kaplan (Program Manager) will issue your permit.
</li>
<li>
<strong>Present your proposal to the Project Director</strong><br>
Before or shortly after the add/drop deadline, share your proposal with <a href="mailto:{{ site.data.contacts.project_director.email }}">{{ site.data.contacts.project_director.name }}</a> (in-person or via email). This ensures the Project Director is aligned on your scope before you start — it is not just a formality. The permit will not be issued until this step is complete.
</li>
</ol>

<div class="info-box warning">
<strong>Timing matters.</strong> Aim to complete steps 1–6 <strong>before</strong> the add/drop deadline. Check the <a href="{{ '/timeline/' | relative_url }}">Timeline</a> for current semester dates.
</div>

## What Makes a Good Project

Not all project ideas are equally well-scoped. Strong projects are:

- **Open-ended** — there is no predetermined answer; you have to figure it out
- **Research-driven** — the work involves genuine analytical or methodological decisions, not just implementation
- **Decomposable** — you can break it into milestones and make measurable progress each month
- **Deep enough** — the core problem can sustain a full semester (or two) of focused work

Projects that tend to struggle:

- Pure data cleaning or pipeline work with no analytical output
- "Just implement X and apply it" — a pre-specified algorithm with no research component
- Consulting-style deliverables (dashboards, reports) without a research question underneath
- Projects where the answer is already known and you're only verifying it

If you're unsure whether your idea crosses the line, email the Project Director before committing to an advisor.

## What to Expect in the First Few Weeks

After enrollment, the first four weeks should focus on:

1. **Literature and context** — read papers in your area; understand what already exists and where your project fits
2. **Data and feasibility** — identify your data sources, check access, and run preliminary experiments to validate your approach is tractable
3. **Refine your research questions** — use what you learn in steps 1–2 to sharpen the questions in your proposal
4. **Establish a working rhythm with your advisor** — agree on meeting cadence, how you'll share progress, and what a good update looks like

Don't wait until month two to discover that your data doesn't exist or your approach doesn't work. The first few weeks are for finding out.

## Choosing Between Thesis and Practicum

**Choose Thesis (DATS 9990) if** you want to lead an entire research project from start to finish, your work could result in a peer-reviewed publication, and you're ready for a two-semester commitment.

**Choose Practicum (DATS 5990) if** you want to contribute meaningfully to a research project (potentially as part of a larger team) or prefer a shorter engagement (one semester, one CU). Industry-based projects are also an option here, as long as they produce an academically evaluable product.
